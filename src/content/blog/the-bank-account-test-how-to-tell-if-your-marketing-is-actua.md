### The Bank-Account Test: The Only Metric That Matters

The Bank-Account Test is simple: **Does the money hitting your account at the end of the month significantly outweigh the money you paid the agency and the ad spend?**

If the answer is "I'm not sure," then your marketing is failing. Not because the ads aren't running, but because you don't have a system to tie a specific dollar spent to a specific job booked.

In the trades, we don't care about "engagement." We care about:
1. The phone ringing.
2. Qualified leads you actually own (not rented leads from a third party).
3. Jobs on the calendar.
4. Cash in the bank.

Anything else is just noise.

### How to Spot "Agency Fluff" Before It Costs You Thousands

When an agency tries to sell you, or when they send you a monthly report, they will try to distract you with "Vanity Metrics." These are numbers that look impressive but don't pay your mortgage.

**The Red Flags:**
*   **"Impressions":** This just means your ad appeared on someone's screen. It doesn't mean they saw it, liked it, or are even in your service area.
*   **"CTR (Click-Through Rate)":** This means people clicked. Great. But did they click because they wanted a quote for a new roof, or did they click by accident while scrolling on their phone?
*   **"Website Traffic":** More visitors are useless if they are "window shoppers" or people looking for a job.

**The Green Flags (The Only Things You Should Ask About):**
*   **Booked Jobs:** "How many of these leads actually scheduled a service call?"
*   **Cost Per Booked Job:** "I spent $1,000 this month. I got 10 jobs. It cost me $100 to get each job on the calendar. Is that profitable?"
*   **Phone Call Volume:** "How many times did the phone ring specifically because of this campaign?"

### The Math of a Profitable Shop

One of the biggest stressors for a business owner is knowing how much to spend. You don't want to starve the business, but you don't want to get ripped off. 

Marketing isn't a "cost"—it's an investment. If you spend $1 to make $5, you do that until you can't handle any more work. But you need a benchmark to keep your agency accountable.

For most local service businesses, here is the reality of the numbers:

**For shops doing under $1M in annual revenue:**
You should typically allocate **5% to 10% of your gross revenue** toward marketing. If you're doing $500k a year, spending $25k–$50k annually to keep the pipeline full is a healthy target. If you're spending 20% and the phone isn't ringing, you're overpaying for poor results.

**For shops doing $1M to $3M in annual revenue:**
As you scale and your overhead grows (more crews, bigger shop, more equipment), your marketing spend usually shifts to **8% to 12% of revenue**. At this stage, you aren't just looking for "any" job; you're looking for the *most profitable* jobs to maximize your crew's efficiency.

If your agency can't tell you exactly where you sit in these percentages—and more importantly, how to move the needle on the revenue side—they aren't managing your growth; they're just managing your budget.

### How to Take Control of Your Lead Flow

If you're tired of paying for "leads" that are shared with four other guys in your zip code, it's time to pivot. You need to own your lead generation.

When you own the lead, you aren't paying a middleman for a "chance" at a job. You are building an asset. Here is how you apply the Bank-Account Test to your current strategy:

#### 1. Audit Your Call Log
Stop looking at the Google Ads dashboard. Look at your phone log. Did you get 20 calls this week? How many of those were "tire kickers" and how many were "I need this fixed today" calls? If the agency says they sent you 100 leads, but you only had 10 phone calls, the agency is lying to you.

#### 2. Track the "Calendar Conversion"
A lead is not a win. A booked job is a win. 
*   **Lead:** Someone fills out a form. (Value: $0)
*   **Booked Job:** A date and time are set for your crew to show up. (Value: Potential Revenue)

If your agency is bragging about "leads," ask them why those leads aren't booking. If the leads are garbage, the marketing is garbage. Period.

#### 3. Demand Geographic Accuracy
If you're a contractor in Arlington, you don't want leads from Plano if you're not willing to drive that far. Many agencies use "broad targeting" to make their numbers look better. They'll tell you they got you "more reach," but that reach is happening in a city you don't service. That's not growth; that's a waste of your money.
